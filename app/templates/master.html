In the wake of a disaster, there are millions of communications, either direct or via social media. But they happen right when disaster response organizations have the least capacity to filter and pull out the most critical messages. Furthermore, only one in every thousand messages is relevant to the disaster response professionals, according to Robert Munro, former CTO of Figure Eight (now Appen).


Disasters are typically responded to by different organizations that take care of different parts of the problem. So one organization will care about water, another will care about blocked roads, another will care about medical supplies. But sorting through help requests by keywords only is ineffective. For example, when you look at a keyword like "water," that's a tiny percent of the time that it will map to someone who needs fresh drinking water. Moreover, that will miss people who say they're thirsty but don't use the word "water."


To tackle this problem, this project uses thousands of pre-labeled tweets and text messages from real-life disasters to train a supervised machine learning model. The model then predicts what the message is about, so that each aid organization gets its correspondent help request. More details and source code is available here.
